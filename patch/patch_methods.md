# Synthetic Dye Overlay Methods

To simulate herbicide dye on untreated vegetation during model training, we developed a synthetic overlay pipeline grounded in empirical color measurements from real dye applications. For each sprayed tile in the training set (0.5-meter patches only), we computed the mean hue, saturation, and value (HSV) of a randomly selected 16×16-pixel region (matching the vision transformer patch dimensions) within 0.125 meters of the spray centroid, where dye coverage is most concentrated, as well as the mean HSV of a randomly selected peripheral region of the same dimensions located fully outside the spray bounds. We used circular statistics to average hue values, accounting for the circular nature of the hue dimension. The HSV delta between paired dye and vegetation regions constituted a single observation per tile, yielding one paired measurement for each of approximately 157 red and 158 blue tiles across all three survey months (May, July, October), pooled by season. We estimated the 25th and 75th percentile of the delta distributions via bootstrap resampling (1,000 iterations with replacement), taking the median of bootstrapped quantile estimates for stability. During training, we overlaid 1 to 10 synthetic dye patches per tile at random locations with 50% probability. Each patch was a roughly circular shape (0.1–1.0 m diameter) with slight boundary irregularity produced by superimposing 2–3 sinusoidal harmonics on the radial boundary in polar coordinates, approximating the appearance of hand-sprayed herbicide. For each synthetic patch, we independently sampled HSV shift values uniformly from the bootstrapped interquartile range of the corresponding dye color's delta distribution and applied these shifts additively to each pixel's HSV representation, with hue wrapping circularly and saturation and value clamped to the unit interval.
